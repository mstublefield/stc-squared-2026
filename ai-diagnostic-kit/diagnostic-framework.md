# Team Diagnostic Framework — Reference

## Overview

This framework uses five diagnostic lenses to reveal invisible patterns killing team productivity. Each lens shows something the others can't. Together, they give a complete picture of what's actually happening on a team — not what everyone thinks is happening.

The core insight: **the gap between what teams say is happening and what's actually happening is where every delivery problem lives.** You can't see this gap from inside. It's like trying to read the label from inside the bottle.

---

## Lens 1: How Your Team Spends Time Together

### What This Lens Reveals
How time is actually allocated versus how people think it's allocated. There's almost always a gap. Teams that feel busy but can't deliver usually have a time allocation problem they can't see.

### Phase 1 Question
"When someone on your team has a complex problem to solve, what usually happens?"
- A) They have blocks of uninterrupted time to think through it.
- B) They work on it between meetings and messages throughout the day.
- C) They're lucky to get 30 minutes without an interruption.
- D) I'm not sure how my team's time actually breaks down.

### Phase 2 Questions
1. "How many hours in a typical day does your team spend in meetings versus doing focused work?"
   - A) Meetings take up less than 25% of the day.
   - B) It's roughly 50/50.
   - C) Meetings dominate — 60% or more of the day.
   - D) Let me describe what a typical day looks like.

2. "Think about your team's meetings last week. How many of them produced a decision or moved work forward?"
   - A) Most of them — we're pretty disciplined about meeting purpose.
   - B) Some did, some were status updates that could have been async.
   - C) Honestly, most of them felt like going through the motions.
   - D) Let me think about this...

3. "Does your team have protected time for deep work — time that's blocked off and respected?"
   - A) Yes, and people actually use it.
   - B) It's on the calendar but gets overridden regularly.
   - C) No — the calendar is a wall-to-wall grid.
   - D) I'm not sure what my team's calendar actually looks like day to day.

### Patterns

**The Meeting Trap:** When teams spend most of their time in synchronous meetings, they confuse motion with progress. Everyone feels busy and engaged. The data shows nothing is getting done. This is one of the most common and most invisible patterns — because the meetings feel productive.

**The Fragmentation Problem:** Even without excessive meetings, constant interruptions (messages, quick questions, "got a minute?") destroy the ability to do complex work. Research shows it takes 23 minutes to refocus after an interruption. A team with 10 interruptions a day has almost no time for deep work.

**The Status Update Loop:** Teams hold daily standups, weekly syncs, and monthly reviews — and none of them produce decisions. They've become rituals that everyone attends but nobody finds valuable. People won't say this publicly because "nobody wants to be the person who cancels the meeting."

### Blind Spot Guidance (for D answers)
- Track your own time for one week. Just note when you're in meetings, doing focused work, or handling interruptions. The numbers will surprise you.
- Ask three teammates: "How much of your day is meetings versus actual work?" Compare their answers to each other and to what you expected.

### Concrete Suggestions
- Pick one day this week and count: how many hours in meetings, how many in focused work? Just count. Don't try to fix anything yet.
- Look at your team's calendar for last week. Identify the meetings that produced a decision. How many were there?
- Try canceling one recurring meeting for two weeks and see if anyone notices or if work suffers. If neither happens, it wasn't needed.
- Block 2 hours tomorrow as "focus time" and see if it survives the day intact. That tells you something about your team's culture.

### Case Example
One team was spending eight hours a day in synchronous video calls. Product was defining requirements while design was creating visuals while engineering was writing code — all simultaneously. They called it collaboration. Nobody had time to think. Product couldn't plan ahead. Design couldn't iterate. Engineering couldn't focus. The meetings felt productive but destroyed the team's ability to do actual work. When meeting time was reduced to about 20% of the work week, productivity tripled within 90 days.

## Lens 2: How Information Actually Flows

### What This Lens Reveals
Every team has two conversations happening at once. The public conversation — what people say in meetings, shared channels, and reports. And the private conversation — what people say in DMs, hallway chats, and to their spouse after work. The distance between those two conversations is the size of the problem.

### Phase 1 Question
"When a deadline feels unrealistic, what does your team typically do?"
- A) Someone raises it publicly and the timeline gets adjusted.
- B) People express concerns privately but go along publicly.
- C) Everyone just puts their head down and tries to make it work.
- D) I'm not sure how my team handles this.

### Phase 2 Questions
1. "When's the last time someone on your team publicly disagreed with a decision or commitment?"
   - A) This week — we debate things openly.
   - B) I can think of a time, but it's been a while.
   - C) I honestly can't remember the last time.
   - D) Let me think about this...

2. "Where do important decisions actually get made on your team?"
   - A) In meetings where everyone's present.
   - B) In side conversations between 2-3 people, then announced to the group.
   - C) It's unclear — decisions seem to just happen and nobody knows when they were made.
   - D) Let me describe how decisions work.

3. "If something is going wrong on a project, how long does it take for the right people to hear about it?"
   - A) Quickly — within a day.
   - B) A few days to a week.
   - C) Sometimes weeks, or it doesn't surface until it's a crisis.
   - D) I'm not sure — I might be one of the people who hears about it late.

### Patterns

**The Agreement Illusion:** Public agreement masking private disagreement. Everyone nods in the meeting. Nobody believes the plan will work. Leadership is blindsided when commitments are missed because the team was signaling agreement the whole time. This isn't dishonesty — it's a response to a culture where disagreeing feels unsafe.

**The Shadow Decision Network:** Important decisions get made in informal side conversations, then presented to the team as already decided. People feel excluded and disengaged because by the time they hear about a decision, it's too late to influence it. The formal decision-making process is theater.

**The Information Lag:** Bad news takes too long to reach leadership. By the time a problem surfaces, it's a crisis instead of a correction. Teams learn to protect leadership from bad news because the messenger gets blamed — so they stop delivering the message.

### Blind Spot Guidance (for D answers)
- After your next team meeting, ask one person privately: "Was there anything in that meeting that didn't feel right to you but you didn't say?" Listen to the answer carefully.
- Pay attention to DMs and private channels after a public decision. If there's a spike of private conversation after a meeting, people are saying privately what they didn't say publicly.

### Concrete Suggestions
- In your next team meeting, explicitly ask: "Does anyone disagree with this plan? I need to hear it now, not in three weeks." Then wait. The silence is data.
- Pick the last missed deadline. Trace backward: when did you first know it was at risk? When did leadership know? The gap between those two dates is your information flow problem.
- Ask a trusted teammate: "When something's going wrong, do people feel comfortable raising it early, or do they wait?" The answer tells you about your culture.
- Look at the last three decisions your team made. For each one, can you point to the meeting or conversation where it was actually decided? If not, your decision-making process is informal and invisible.

### Case Example
At one company, engineers were publicly agreeing to deadlines while privately telling each other the timelines were impossible. Leadership had no idea. They kept setting aggressive deadlines, the team kept agreeing, and then the team kept missing them. Leadership thought the problem was execution. The real problem was that the team didn't feel safe saying "this won't work" in public.

## Lens 3: How Work Moves Through Your System

### What This Lens Reveals
Follow one unit of work from start to finish — not the idealized process on your wiki, but the actual path a real piece of work takes. Most of the elapsed time is waiting, not working. A task that takes two days of actual effort takes three weeks to finish because it's sitting in a queue, waiting for a review, blocked on a question nobody asked.

### Phase 1 Question
"Think about the last thing your team delivered. How much of the total time was actual work versus waiting, handoffs, or rework?"
- A) Mostly work — things moved steadily from start to finish.
- B) Roughly half and half — some waiting, but work kept moving.
- C) Mostly waiting — a lot of time spent in queues, handoffs, or redoing things.
- D) I'm not sure — I haven't looked at it that way.

### Phase 2 Questions
1. "When work gets stuck on your team, where does it usually happen?"
   - A) Waiting for requirements or clarity on what to build.
   - B) Waiting for review, testing, or approval.
   - C) Waiting for another team or external dependency.
   - D) Let me describe what happens.

2. "How often does work start before the requirements are clear — and then need to be redone?"
   - A) Rarely — we have a clear definition of ready before work starts.
   - B) Sometimes — maybe 20-30% of work needs rework.
   - C) Frequently — rework is a constant problem.
   - D) I'm not sure how much of our effort goes to rework.

3. "If you traced a single task through your system, how many people or handoffs would it touch before it was done?"
   - A) 2-3 people — pretty streamlined.
   - B) 4-6 people — a few handoffs but manageable.
   - C) 7+ people or I've lost count — it's a long chain.
   - D) Let me think about what the path actually looks like.

### Patterns

**Premature Execution:** Work starts before requirements are clear. The team builds something, discovers it's not right, tears it down, and starts over. Every task becomes two tasks. Rework consumes 30-60% of capacity, but nobody tracks it because it looks like "normal" work. This is one of the most expensive patterns a team can have.

**The Handoff Tax:** Every handoff introduces delay and information loss. A task that's "in progress" for three weeks might have only two days of actual work — the rest is waiting for someone to pick it up, review it, approve it, or answer a question about it. The more handoffs, the more elapsed time, the more things fall through the cracks.

**The Queue Problem:** Work piles up at bottlenecks. The team keeps starting new work (because waiting feels unproductive) while existing work sits in a queue. Work-in-progress grows, cycle time grows, and throughput actually drops. More work started means less work finished.

### Blind Spot Guidance (for D answers)
- Pick one piece of work your team recently completed. Write down every step it went through — every handoff, every review, every time it sat waiting. The actual number of steps will be higher than you expect.
- Ask your team: "What's the oldest thing in our backlog that's currently in progress?" The answer tells you about your cycle time.

### Concrete Suggestions
- Pick one piece of work that's currently in progress. Ask everyone involved: "Do we all agree on what done looks like?" If the answer is no, you just found your bottleneck.
- Map the journey of your last completed deliverable: start date, every handoff, every wait, finish date. Count the days of actual work versus days of waiting. The ratio tells you everything.
- Look at your work management system right now. How many things are "in progress"? If it's more than 2-3 per person, you have a WIP problem — and finishing one thing will be faster than starting another.
- For the next piece of work that starts, ask: "Are the requirements clear enough that someone could build this without coming back to ask questions?" If not, it's not ready to start.

### Case Example
One team had tickets sitting "in progress" for weeks. Work was starting before requirements were clear, so engineers would build something, find out it wasn't right, and start over. Constant rework. The tickets said "in progress" but the reality was "waiting for someone to figure out what we're actually supposed to build." When the team stopped starting work before requirements were clear, cycle time dropped from six weeks to five days.

## Lens 4: What the Numbers Actually Say

### What This Lens Reveals
The data is usually there — it's just not being looked at the right way. Or the wrong metrics are getting tracked. Velocity looks great while cycle time is terrible. Story points go up while shipped features are flat. The numbers don't lie, but they do get ignored — especially when they tell a story leadership doesn't want to hear.

### Phase 1 Question
"Is your team shipping more or less than they were six months ago?"
- A) More — we're trending in the right direction.
- B) About the same, even though we've added people or effort.
- C) Less — despite more people, more hours, or more process.
- D) I'm not sure — we don't really track this.

### Phase 2 Questions
1. "What metrics does your team currently track?"
   - A) Cycle time, throughput, and quality metrics.
   - B) Velocity or story points and sprint completion.
   - C) We track activity (tickets closed, PRs merged) but not outcomes.
   - D) Let me describe what we measure.

2. "What percentage of your team's effort goes into rework — fixing bugs, redoing work, or correcting things that weren't right the first time?"
   - A) Less than 15% — quality is generally high.
   - B) Maybe 20-40% — it's noticeable but not the main problem.
   - C) 40% or more — it feels like we're constantly fixing things.
   - D) I don't know — we don't track this.

3. "When the numbers tell a story that's uncomfortable — like declining output or long cycle times — what happens?"
   - A) We address it directly and look for root causes.
   - B) We acknowledge it but struggle to fix it.
   - C) It gets explained away or blamed on external factors.
   - D) Let me describe what usually happens.

### Patterns

**The Vanity Metrics Trap:** Tracking metrics that feel good but don't measure what matters. Velocity is up! (But cycle time is four weeks and nothing's actually shipping.) Story points completed! (But half of those are bug fixes from the previous sprint.) Activity metrics create the illusion of progress while the real indicators — cycle time, throughput, defect rate — go unexamined.

**The Brooks's Law Spiral:** Adding people makes the team slower, not faster. More people means more coordination overhead, more meetings, more handoffs. Throughput declines while headcount grows. Leadership's instinct is to hire more — but the system can't absorb more people. The constraint isn't capacity; it's coordination.

**The Rework Tax:** A significant portion of the team's capacity goes to redoing work that wasn't right the first time. Nobody tracks rework separately, so it's invisible in the metrics. A team that spends 50% of its time on rework effectively has half the capacity it thinks it has.

### Blind Spot Guidance (for D answers)
- Count how many things your team shipped in the last month. Then count the same number for three months ago. The trend tells a story.
- Ask your team lead: "What percentage of our work is rework versus new work?" Most teams dramatically underestimate this.
- If you don't track cycle time, take your last 10 completed items and calculate the average time from start to finish. That number is your baseline.

### Concrete Suggestions
- Calculate one number this week: average cycle time. Take your last 10 completed tasks, note the start date and finish date for each, and average them. If the number surprises you, you've found something.
- Count your team's output this month. Not effort, not activity — count the things that actually shipped to users or customers. Compare to six months ago.
- Ask: "What metrics are we tracking, and are they measuring what matters or just what's easy to measure?" If the team can't connect their metrics to customer or business outcomes, the metrics are probably vanity metrics.
- Look at your bug or defect count over the last quarter. Is it growing, shrinking, or flat? A growing defect count is a leading indicator that quality problems are compounding.

### Case Example
One team's cycle time was four to six weeks per task when it should have been five days. Sixty percent of engineering capacity was being consumed by rework from unclear requirements. And despite adding engineers, throughput was declining — more people, less output. The data told a clear story: the team wasn't constrained by capacity. They were constrained by a system that generated rework faster than they could absorb it.

## Lens 5: What People Believe vs. What They Say Out Loud

### What This Lens Reveals
The distance between what people tell leadership and what they actually believe is the biggest blind spot in any organization. This isn't about dishonesty — people aren't lying. They're responding rationally to a culture where disagreeing feels unsafe, raising concerns feels pointless, or where they've learned that the messenger gets blamed.

### Phase 1 Question
"If your team could tell leadership one thing anonymously, do you think it would be surprising?"
- A) No — leadership already knows what the team thinks.
- B) Maybe — there are a few things people don't say out loud.
- C) Absolutely — there's a big gap between what's said and what's believed.
- D) I'm not sure — I might be on the wrong side of this gap to see it.

### Phase 2 Questions
1. "When was the last time someone on your team raised a concern that actually changed a decision?"
   - A) Recently — feedback regularly influences decisions.
   - B) It's happened, but it's rare — most decisions feel pre-made.
   - C) I can't think of a time — people have stopped trying.
   - D) Let me describe how feedback works on our team.

2. "Do people on your team believe the current plan will work?"
   - A) Yes — there's genuine confidence in the direction.
   - B) Mixed — some believe it, some don't, nobody talks about the gap.
   - C) No — but people are going through the motions anyway.
   - D) I haven't actually asked them.

3. "What happens when someone raises a concern or disagrees with a decision?"
   - A) It gets heard and discussed — disagreement is normal.
   - B) It gets acknowledged but nothing changes — people learn to stop raising things.
   - C) It gets dismissed or the person gets labeled as negative — people learn to stay quiet.
   - D) Let me describe what I've seen.

### Patterns

**The Silence Tax:** When people stop raising concerns, leadership loses its early warning system. Problems that could have been caught in a conversation become crises that take weeks to fix. The team isn't quiet because everything's fine — they're quiet because they've learned that speaking up doesn't help.

**The Confidence Gap:** Leadership believes the plan will work. The team doesn't. But nobody is talking about the gap. This leads to missed deadlines that "nobody saw coming" — except the team saw it coming months ago. They just didn't feel like it was safe or useful to say so.

**The Learned Helplessness Loop:** The team has raised concerns before. Nothing changed. So they stopped raising concerns. Leadership interprets the silence as agreement. The cycle reinforces itself — less feedback leads to worse decisions leads to worse outcomes leads to more frustration leads to even less feedback.

### Blind Spot Guidance (for D answers)
- Ask one trusted person on your team: "Is there anything about how we work that you think leadership doesn't see?" Listen without defending.
- Pay attention in your next few meetings: is anyone noticeably quiet who used to speak up? That change in behavior is a data point.
- D on this question is particularly significant — if you're not sure whether there's a gap between what people say and what they believe, you're probably on the leadership side of that gap. Finding out requires asking, not guessing.

### Concrete Suggestions
- This week, ask one person on your team: "What's the thing everyone knows but nobody says out loud?" Then listen. Don't defend. Don't explain. Just listen.
- In your next meeting, after a decision is made, try: "I want to hear from anyone who disagrees or has concerns. This is the time." Then wait through the uncomfortable silence. If someone eventually speaks, you've just created safety.
- Think about the last time someone raised a concern. What happened to them? Were they heard? Was anything changed? If the answer is no, that's why people stopped raising concerns.
- Ask your team directly: "Do you believe our current plan will work?" If you're a leader, you might need to ask this anonymously (a quick survey, not a public meeting) to get an honest answer.

### Case Example
At one company, an engineer said something that unlocked the entire diagnostic: "We're coding before we understand what we're building." That single insight explained the 80% defect rate, the six-week cycle times, the constant rework. The engineer had known this for months. Nobody had asked. The team had learned that raising concerns didn't change anything — so they stopped raising them.

## Pattern Library: Cross-Lens Combinations

These are common patterns that emerge when multiple lenses show related signals.

### Synchronous Chaos
**Signals:** Lens 1 (C) + Lens 2 (B or C) + Lens 4 (C)
Excessive meeting time combined with private disagreement and declining output. The team is trapped in meetings all day, can't think, can't push back on the schedule, and results are getting worse. This pattern feels productive to leadership because everyone's "collaborating." The data shows nothing's getting done.

### Premature Execution Spiral
**Signals:** Lens 3 (C) + Lens 4 (B or C) + Lens 5 (B or C)
Work moves slowly because it starts before it's ready. The team knows requirements aren't clear but doesn't feel safe pushing back. Rework consumes capacity. Output declines. This pattern is expensive and self-reinforcing — the more rework there is, the more pressure there is to start the next thing quickly, which generates more rework.

### The Trust Breakdown
**Signals:** Lens 2 (B or C) + Lens 5 (B or C) + Lens 4 (B or C)
Information isn't flowing honestly. People don't believe the plan will work but aren't saying so. And the numbers confirm that something is broken. This is a leadership and culture problem, not a process problem. No framework or tool will fix it — the team has to rebuild the ability to tell the truth to each other.

### The Invisible Bottleneck
**Signals:** Lens 3 (C) + Lens 1 (B or C) + Lens 4 (B or C)
Work gets stuck in the system, time is consumed by meetings or interruptions, and output is flat or declining. There's a specific bottleneck somewhere — often in handoffs, unclear requirements, or overburdened reviewers — but it's hidden because the team is busy and feels productive. The data tells a different story.

### The Autopilot Problem
**Signals:** Lens 1 (B) + Lens 2 (B) + Lens 3 (B) + Lens 4 (B) + Lens 5 (B)
Everything is "fine." Nothing is great. The team is running on autopilot — not in crisis, but not improving. This is the hardest pattern to address because there's no obvious pain point. The danger is gradual decline: each quarter is slightly worse than the last, but the change is too slow to trigger alarm.
